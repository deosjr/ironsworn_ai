# Campaign Setup — Lewis of Mervik, Lord of Hammarby

## Inspirations

*Strong:*
- Twin Peaks (David Lynch) — the rural uncanny, ancient evil operating beneath ordinary life, the Log Lady figure, atmosphere as information
- True Detective Season 1 (Nic Pizzolatto) — the investigation that opens onto something institutional and cosmic, the protagonist whose perception makes him illegible, corruption maintained by the people appointed to stop it

*Considered:*
- The Buried Giant (Kazuo Ishiguro) — collective memory as active suppression, the past surfacing despite everything, what happens when a society chooses to forget
- Njáls saga — slow-burning consequence, honour culture, one injustice generating the next
- The Name of the Rose (Umberto Eco) — forbidden knowledge, institutional suppression, the horror of what official religion refuses to acknowledge
- The Wicker Man (1973) — an outsider encountering a closed community with its own intact relationship to the old ways
- The Secret History (Donna Tartt) — collective complicity, the group becoming something none of its members intended alone

## Historical & Mythological Grounding

**The Harrying of the North (1069-70)**
William I's systematic destruction of northern England — burning crops, salting fields, killing livestock — left the region depopulated for a generation. Lewis's estate is a direct consequence: land cleared of its previous owners, granted to a loyal soldier who has to make something of the wreckage. The Harrying is not backstory. It is the active wound the campaign inhabits.

**Sacred Kingship**
The engine of the fifth season. The anthropological concept — most famously articulated in Frazer's *The Golden Bough* — of a king whose body is identified with the land's fertility. His ritual participation in seasonal cycles maintains the boundary between worlds. His refusal breaks it. The Conqueror king's refusal is not villainy. It is the collision of two incompatible cosmologies, with the world caught in between.

**Roman Britain / Norman England synthesis**
Two moments of conquest layered together. The Conquerors carry Roman civic polytheism — pragmatic, administrative, tolerant of native gods as long as they're renamed and subordinated. The Natives carry Celtic/Anglo-Saxon religious practice — druids, old gods, seasonal ritual, the land as sacred body. The tension is simultaneously religious, political, and cosmic.

**Geography: Uppsala region, Sweden**
Modelled on the landscape around Linnaeus' Hammarby, southeast of Uppsala. Low rolling terrain, mixed forest, open fields between tree lines, lakes. Quiet and melancholy. The geography of a place that remembers things.

## Core Themes

**The rural uncanny**
The horror is already there. It was always there. The mist that doesn't lift, the dry grass under a dead man, the field that the peasants won't approach. The fifth season isn't an intrusion — it's a revelation of what was always underneath. Lewis's estate sits on top of something old and the conquest disturbed it without understanding what it was disturbing.

**Institutional blindness as active danger**
The Conqueror king is not evil. He is certain. His certainty — that the old religion is barbarism, that the Harrying was total victory, that the matter is closed — is more dangerous than malice because it cannot be argued with. The queen is more dangerous still: she sees something is wrong but frames it entirely in political terms, which means her response makes the real problem worse.

**The cost of being in between**
Lewis is a lowborn soldier elevated into the conqueror class. He belongs fully to neither world. The natives don't trust him — he came with the Harrying. The conquerors don't fully accept him — he has no surname, no armour, no family history. This positioning is his only advantage and his constant vulnerability. The tragedy of the campaign is that brokering the collaboration that could avert the fifth season may require him to become something neither side will forgive.

**Small gestures, large consequences**
Lewis sat on a small stool in Edith's home. He listened instead of threatened. He buried Aldric himself. These small choices are the real progress of the campaign — not battles or vows fulfilled, but moments where a conqueror behaved like a human being to people he had every institutional reason to treat as subjects. The fifth season will not be averted by a single heroic act. It will be averted, if it is averted at all, by the accumulation of these moments.

**The vow as burden**
Lewis's inciting vow is practical — make the field safe before harvest. But it opened onto something much larger than a field. The background vow of establishing his name sits over everything, and it is becoming clear that the name worth having cannot be earned by the means available to a conqueror lord. What Lewis's version of restitution looks like — whether it's even possible — is the question the campaign is slowly approaching.

## The World

### The Fifth Season
The native culture practiced sacred kingship — an annual ritual in which the king wed a chosen maiden, and the maiden was sacrificed in autumn to ensure the seasonal cycle continued. This kept the boundary between the living world and the underworld sealed.

The Conqueror king refused. The Harrying was total victory; the old religion is barbarism; the matter is closed.

The boundary is thinning. A fifth season — of endless mist — grows longer each year. Things leak through: not things you can fight, not things you can bury and be done with. Strange, unknowable presences. The thin places are where it is worst — locations where the boundary has worn through entirely. Aldric died at one. There will be more.

Thin places can be sealed individually through localised ritual — something given willingly to what lies beneath, performed by someone who knows the old ways. This does not address the fifth season. It addresses only its local symptoms.

The fifth season has happened before in myth. No living person has seen it or knows how it ended.

### The Factions

**The Conqueror King** — declared victory, willfully blind, will resist any information contradicting his narrative of total control. Not a villain. Certain, which is worse.

**The Queen** — the most dangerous figure. Does not share her husband's complacency. Has caught wind of occult whispers and believes the druids represent a real threat — framing it as political rather than cosmic. Under cover of a national survey (equivalent of the Domesday Book), she is sending agents across the land to wage a shadow war against the druids. She is a believer who probably does not understand the true nature of what she is fighting. Her inquisition makes the real problem worse.

**The Druids** — keepers of the old ritual. Not villains — functionaries of something larger than themselves, increasingly desperate. They would collaborate with the Conquerors if trust were possible. It is not — yet. One druid knows a rite to seal thin places and has done so before. She is coming to Hammarby when she is ready.

**The Displaced Natives** — an informal network of people dispossessed by the Harrying. They survive in the margins. They watch. They share information. How organised they are is unclear, but *we are everywhere* was said with conviction rather than bluster.

**The Displaced Native Army** — north of the border. Resentful, eager to reconquer their homeland, currently powerless. A pressure that could become kinetic.

### Character Notes
Lewis is blunt, loyal, not particularly subtle. He leads through presence and consistency. People follow him because he does not ask them to do things he would not do himself, and because he keeps his word. That last part matters to him more than he lets on.

The Dale Cooper parallel is exact: Cooper works because his empirical rigor leads him straight into accepting things that would break a less grounded man. He doesn't believe in the Lodge because he abandoned rationality — he believes because the evidence is there and he follows it. Lewis is the same: a practical soldier who sat in Edith's house and took her seriously because what she said fit what he'd seen. Not credulous. Just honest about what's in front of him. Lewis is gruffer than Cooper — the warmth comes out in actions rather than words, in burying Aldric himself and carrying the prisoner home rather than leaving him in the dark.

He is quietly troubled by what he participated in during the Harrying. He does not admit this. He has filed it under *soldiers follow orders*, which works until it doesn't. The repentance piece of his background vow has no shape yet. He has not admitted to himself that it is there. It is there nonetheless.

## Vows

### Background Vow — *I will establish my name* (Epic)
Build Hammarby into an estate worthy of a lord. Acquire armour befitting a knight. Learn to ride properly. And — less clearly articulated, even to himself — find some form of restitution for what was done in the Harrying. In whatever small scope he can manage.

The repentance piece has no shape yet. It is there nonetheless.

**Progress: 0/40**

### Inciting Vow — *I will find what killed Aldric and make the north field safe before harvest* (Formidable)
Aldric — a soldier under Lewis's command, known personally — found dead at the north field's edge. No wound. No struggle. His heart stopped when something from beneath looked at him directly. The field is a thin place. The peasants will not go near it. If it remains untilled, the estate cannot produce enough to fund rebuilding.

The vow is practical as much as personal. Lewis needs that field. He also needs to know what killed a man he trusted.

**Progress: 1/10**
**Current lead:** Conlaoch has agreed to bring a druid to neutral ground. She will come when she is ready — on her schedule, not Lewis's.

## Key Relationships (After Session 2)

**Edith** — oldest woman in the village, present since before the Harrying. The other peasants defer to her without making a show of it. Sharp-eyed, composed, not easily frightened. Told Lewis about the fifth season and the north field. Not an ally yet — but common ground exists. She knows more than she said. The Log Lady of Hammarby.

**Theodor** — Lewis's administrator. Competent, careful, quietly opinionated. Now knows everything Lewis knows. His counsel: handle the druid quietly, keep no record, one thing at a time. Can write official reports in a way that deflects scrutiny. Lewis trusts him as much as he trusts anyone, which is not entirely. Will not absorb the risk of the queen's actual agent — his loyalty to Lewis has survival limits he won't apologise for.

**Conlaoch** — a native survivor of the Harrying. Lost his wife, children, and farm. Cold, precise, not interested in Lewis's goodwill. An arrangement exists: he will bring a druid to seal the field; Lewis allows the village food at the treeline to continue openly. Neither man trusts the other. Both need something the other can provide. He omitted the network of displaced natives watching the estate. That omission sits uneasily. Lewis mangled his name; Conlaoch let it go.

**Rolf** — oldest of the three remaining soldiers, been with Lewis longest. Knew Aldric. Knows more than the others but isn't pushing.

**Edde** — younger, eager. Has been on watch all night with a prisoner he knows nothing about. Tired and curious.

**Maret** — the quiet one. Possibly the most dangerous thinker of the three.

**Bally** — the horse. Functional and slightly absurd. The peasants wince at the name.

**The Younger Scribe** — the other clerc. Not yet developed.

**The Saved General (dormant)** — the connection that earned Lewis Hammarby. Now manages multiple estates in the south and has largely forgotten Lewis exists. Real but effectively inert.

**The Local Baron** — Lewis's immediate feudal superior. Not yet demanding taxes or levy. Not yet developed.

**Oswin (anticipated — Act Two)** — a queen's surveyor. Arrives with a mandate to inventory estates and assess the region as part of the queen's shadow war against the druids. Competent, professionally suspicious, initially condescending toward a newly knighted border lord with a ruined estate. Arrives expecting hostility or performed cooperation. Gets neither.

The Marty Hart to Lewis's Rust Cohle — not stupid, not corrupt, but institutionally loyal in ways that cost him. Resists the uncomfortable conclusions not from ignorance but because accepting them would require abandoning the framework his career is built on. Gets there eventually, because the evidence is there and he is honest enough to follow it.

Critically: he came north already unsettled. He has seen things on the road that don't fit the queen's political framing. He arrived at Hammarby looking, without quite admitting it, for someone who might know more.

Less trust at the start than Marty and Rust — they were partners by assignment; Lewis and Oswin have no such obligation. The surveyor has authority Lewis can't challenge. Lewis has local knowledge Oswin needs but can withhold. The relationship has to be built from nothing, under mutual suspicion, while both perform cooperation for the official record.

Theodor will be present, professional, and carefully unhelpful — answering what is asked, volunteering nothing, absent when the difficult conversations happen. Lewis handles Oswin himself, with Shadow 1. He can't dissemble well and won't try. He gives Oswin something closer to the truth than the mandate prepared him for, carefully chosen. The moment Oswin recalibrates is where the relationship actually begins.

## Story Arc — Retrofitted and Speculative

### Act One: The Estate (Sessions 1-3, current)
*Scale: local. Stakes: personal and practical.*

Lewis arrives at a ruin and tries to make it function. Within three weeks a man is dead and the field that would save the estate is the thing that killed him. He buries Aldric himself. He sits on a small stool in an old woman's house and listens. He goes alone to a standing stone at dusk and holds his temper when every instinct says otherwise. He carries an unconscious man home over one shoulder and tells Theodor everything.

The prisoner is executed — the honest consequence of attacking a lord on a dark path. Lewis doesn't enjoy it. He does it anyway. The network loses one man and gains confirmation that Lewis is not soft. That information travels.

The arc concludes when the druid arrives and performs the sealing ritual. Not a triumph — a transaction, grudging on both sides, with obligations attached. The field is sealed. Something is given willingly to what lies beneath. The north field can be tilled.

What the sealing costs Lewis, and what it reveals about the fifth season, are the questions that open Act Two.

**Milestone:** The inciting vow fulfilled — or fulfilled enough. The north field is safe. Aldric is understood if not avenged. The estate has a chance.

---

### Act Two: The Estate and Beyond (Sessions 4-8, speculative)
*Scale: regional. Stakes: the estate, the village, Oswin, the question of what Lewis owes.*

The practical crisis is resolved. The larger one is clearer now. The fifth season is not a local problem — the north field was one thin place among many. The druid's visit was not a solution, only a patch. And Oswin is coming.

Lewis begins to understand — slowly, because he is not a man who rushes toward uncomfortable conclusions — what the fifth season actually requires. Not a ritual. Not a sealed field. The king's participation. Which is impossible. Which means the fifth season will keep worsening until something else gives way first.

Oswin arrives as pressure and potential resource simultaneously. He is the only Conqueror-side figure who takes the threat seriously, which makes him valuable. He is also an instrument of an apparatus making the threat worse, which makes him dangerous. Lewis has to give him enough to keep him engaged without getting the druid or Conlaoch killed. He cannot use Theodor as a buffer for this. He has to do it himself, with Shadow 1, by being more honest than Oswin expects and more careful than Lewis is naturally inclined to be.

**Key questions this arc answers:**
- What did the sealing ritual cost Lewis, and what did he see?
- What does the druid ask of him beyond the immediate transaction?
- Who is the village contact sustaining Conlaoch — and what do they know?
- How does Oswin's arrival change what Lewis can and cannot do openly?
- Can Lewis bring Oswin far enough along to be useful without getting anyone killed?
- Does Conlaoch's omission of the vengeance faction come up, and what does Lewis do about it?

**The restitution thread begins to take shape.** Lewis cannot undo the Harrying. He can make specific choices about how he treats specific people on specific days. He begins making those choices more deliberately, without quite admitting that is what he is doing. Edith notices. Conlaoch notices. The village notices.

**Milestone:** Lewis forges a bond — with Edith, with Conlaoch, with the village, or with the druid. This is the moment the estate stops being a place he was given and starts being a place he is responsible for.

---

### Act Three: The Wider World (Sessions 9+, highly speculative)
*Scale: national. Stakes: the fifth season, the king, what Lewis is willing to risk.*

The fifth season cannot be ignored much longer. The mist is longer every year. More thin places are opening. The queen's inquisition is finding druids and destroying the people who might broker a solution. The king remains certain.

Lewis is, by this point, positioned between worlds in a way nobody else is — a Conqueror lord who has forged bonds with native people, who knows what the druids know, who has seen the fifth season from the inside. He may be the only person capable of reaching both the druids and someone with access to the king. Oswin, if the Act Two relationship survived, may be that someone.

This is not a role Lewis wanted. He may not survive it.

**The background vow comes into focus.** Establishing his name — the surface meaning — is no longer the point. The name worth having is one attached to something real. What Lewis's version of restitution looks like, at scale, is the question the campaign has been building toward since he sat on that stool.

**The tragic structure:** averting the fifth season requires the king's participation. The king cannot be compelled. The only path is persuasion — and the only person positioned to attempt it is a lowborn soldier with no family name, no armour, and a relationship with the druids that looks, from outside, like treason.

Whether Lewis attempts it, whether it succeeds, and what it costs him are the questions the campaign has been building toward since he listened instead of threatened.

## What Makes This Campaign Distinct from Corm

Lewis leads through presence, loyalty, and consistency — Heart 3. He is blunt but not cold. People follow him because he does what he says he will do. His version of the protagonist's dilemma is external: the institutions and systems he operates within are the problem, and his task is to work against them without losing his position entirely.

Corm leads through precision and observation — Wits 3. He is cold and knows it. His version of the dilemma is internal: the question is whether he becomes what he has been accused of being. The corruption is a gradient he has to monitor in himself.

Lewis's horror is cosmic and communal — the fifth season affects everyone, and the solution requires collective action across enemy lines. Corm's horror is personal and specific — one creature, one vow, one man holding a line.

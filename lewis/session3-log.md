# Session 3 Log — Hammarby
*An Ironsworn solo campaign. Lewis of Mervik, lord of Hammarby.*

---

## Morning

Lewis woke to arguing. Edde and Rolf, outside the hall, over the prisoner's head. Edde wanted him hanged. Rolf was telling him to wait. Maret was leaning against the far corner saying nothing, which was making both of them uneasy.

Lewis shut it down before Edde had finished his sentence. Addressed him directly and turned to go back inside. Edde said it to his back, which took nerve.

*"Aldric deserved better than this."*

The yard went quiet. Lewis told them the man stayed alive, and that was that. Rolf nodded. Edde looked away. Maret pushed off the wall and went back to whatever she had been doing before.

Inside, Theodor had porridge and a list. He set the list aside when Lewis sat down.

*"The man is still alive, I see."*

Lewis told him what he wanted from the prisoner: confirmation that killing him wouldn't be a declaration of war on the forest folk, and whatever the man knew about the network watching the estate. Theodor's counsel: the first question couldn't be answered through the prisoner alone, and the second depended on how much the man actually knew. He noted, carefully, that Conlaoch would know exactly how organised the network was — and had conspicuously not said.

They agreed the prisoner stayed alive until the druid arrived. Then they went through the list.

## The Village

Lewis went to the village to show his face over a field boundary dispute, and to find who had been leaving food for Conlaoch at the treeline.

**Gather Information — Wits (2)**
*Action die: 4. Challenge dice: 6 and 4. Result: 6 vs 6 and 4 — Weak Hit. +1 Momentum (capped at 10).*

The picture came together without Lewis having to ask anyone directly. The man's name was Oskar — an older farmer with a strip in the north field, fallow for two seasons. He had a son who died after the Harrying, not in the violence but in the hard winter that followed. He had been leaving food at the treeline since autumn.

By the time Lewis crossed the yard toward him, Oskar already knew he was coming.

He didn't flee. He set down his tools and waited.

*"I fed him through winter. I'd do it again."*

Lewis asked him why — why take the risk. Oskar looked at him the way you look at someone who has asked a question with an obvious answer.

*"My strip is in the north field."*

He said it like that explained everything. Then: *"Conlaoch said he knew people who might fix what's wrong with that field. He didn't promise anything. But he was the only one saying anything at all."*

That was when Edith walked up.

*"This man did nothing wrong."*

Not asking for clemency. Stating a position.

Lewis told her it was alright, that Oskar would continue. Then he asked what else she was hiding.

*"Many things. Same as you."*

She confirmed the prisoner's name: Bram. Lost people in the Harrying. Not the only one who thought Conlaoch was wasting his time talking to conquerors. If Lewis killed him, some would say he proved them right. She wasn't asking Lewis to spare him — just telling him what it would mean. *When you do it, do it plainly. Don't dress it up as justice. These people know the difference.*

Bram had no family or friends in the village.

Before she left, Edith produced a note from somewhere in her clothing and held it out without ceremony. A meeting place, written in the old tongue, a crude drawing beside it for those who couldn't read it. A formal invitation. She was only the messenger.

Lewis took it back to Theodor, who read it twice and set it on the table.

*"Three days."* He tapped the drawing. *"This is a clearing. Northeast, inside the forest proper. She chose the ground."*

---

## Three Days

Lewis tried the prisoner every evening. Bram did not speak. On the second day Lewis gave up.

The roof timber was sourced. The field dispute stayed settled. Theodor wrote something about soil drainage that would bore anyone who read it into not reading it.

---

## The Clearing

On the third morning Lewis, Rolf, and Bally — with Bram slung over the horse's back — walked northeast into the forest. The paths narrowed quickly. They made small-talk when the mist thickened and the trees pressed in, the way old soldiers do — not about anything, just to remind each other they were still there.

The clearing was circular, too regular to be natural. The mist stayed at the treeline. Saoirse was already there. Conlaoch stood a step behind her left shoulder.

Lewis handed Rolf the reins and approached. He acknowledged Conlaoch with a nod. Conlaoch's eyes went to Bram.

Saoirse looked at the prisoner.

*"You brought a dead man."*

*"He is not dead yet,"* Lewis said.

*"No. But you have already decided."*

She gave her name. Told Lewis he would not say it correctly. He didn't try.

Lewis offered Bram to her — a token of peace, she could have a say in his fate if the man was hers. Conlaoch spoke flatly: Bram was not with them. He was with people who thought they were fools for being here. Saoirse said it was not hers to decide — but if Lewis meant to kill him, do it somewhere else. Not on this ground.

Then Lewis asked about the field. She told him she could seal it, had done it before. But not for nothing.

She needed something retrieved from a tomb northeast of the clearing — deeper in the forest, a thin place older than the conquest. Something had been taken inside when the boundary first began to fail. The dead there would not trouble a man who entered honestly. They would trouble her.

*"You go in. You bring out what you find. I seal your field."*

Lewis asked how he was to survive it. She explained: the field was a wound, uncontrolled. The tomb was contained. It would know the difference between a man who came to take and a man who came to return something. But it would know if he lied to himself.

There was a spring between the clearing and the tomb. He would stop there first.

*"What the spring asks of you is not my business. But you cannot enter the tomb carrying things you have not put down. Most men find it harder than the tomb itself."*

Lewis looked back at Rolf, said a word, and watched him take Bally and Bram back into the trees. Then he told Saoirse he was choosing to trust her, frankly because he had little choice. That if he did this and she helped him, perhaps they mended more wounds than the field.

*"Perhaps,"* she said.

Conlaoch led him north toward the spring, and waited outside.

## The Spring

Lewis knelt at the pool and waited for something to happen. Nothing came. He stared into the dark water and felt despair beginning to creep in — and then his mind drifted, the way minds do when they stop fighting, and he thought about the Harrying.

The pillaging. The killing. The things his fellow soldiers did to the countryside without him so much as thinking of stopping them. He had filed it under soldiers follow orders for long enough that he had stopped feeling the weight of it. He felt it now.

**Face Danger — Heart (3)**
*Action die: 1. Challenge dice: 1 and 8. Result: 4 vs 1 and 8 — Weak Hit. +1 Momentum (capped at 10). Endure Stress (1) — Spirit: 5 → 4.*

He didn't pull back. That was all the spring wanted — not absolution, not penance. Just acknowledgment. He stood up a little hollowed, carrying it openly for the first time.

Con said nothing when Lewis climbed back out of the hollow. *"Northeast. You'll find it."*

## The Journey to the Tomb

**Undertake a Journey — Dangerous**

*Waypoint 1 — Wits (2): Action die: 4. Challenge dice: 9 and 4. Result: 6 vs 9 and 4 — Weak Hit. Progress: 2/10. -1 Supply (now 4).*

The path was real but took longer than it should. Lewis lost it twice, backtracked, burned through provisions sitting still and thinking. He found a flat stone beside a narrow stream — old enough that moss had swallowed the edges, worn smooth by long use. He sat, ate, drank. The tomb mound was visible through the trees.

*Waypoint 2 — Wits (2): Action die: 4. Challenge dice: 9 and 5. Result: 6 vs 9 and 5 — Weak Hit. Progress: 4/10. -1 Supply (now 3).*

The second waypoint was a clearing hung with woven charms in the branches — different ages, some weathered to almost nothing, some newer. Tended over years. A boundary. Lewis didn't touch them.

*Waypoint 3 — Wits (2): Action die: 3. Challenge dice: 10 and 6. Result: 5 vs 10 and 6 — Miss. Pay the Price (rolled 74): Stressful. Endure Stress (1) — Spirit: 4 → 3.*

Beyond the charms the path went wrong. Lewis circled back twice, the forest rearranging itself in the mist. When he found his bearing again he was spooked — aware of how alone he was, unable to tell whether he had crossed into a thin place. He pressed on.

**Reach Your Destination — Progress 4**
*Challenge dice: 7 and 4. Result: Weak Hit. Arrives with a complication.*

The tomb entrance was blocked. Recently — fresh stones packed into the opening, a woven charm across the lintel. Someone had gotten here first and sealed it. The charm was the same type as Conlaoch's warning at the north field.

**Gather Information — Wits (2)**
*Action die: 5. Challenge dice: 5 and 10. Result: 7 vs 5 and 10 — Weak Hit. +1 Momentum (capped at 10).*

The sealing was native work — days old at most. The charm was a warning, not a weapon. The same hand as the north field, possibly. Con had sealed it, or had it sealed.

Lewis started moving stones.

**Face Danger — Iron (2)**
*Action die: 6. Challenge dice: 7 and 7. Result: 8 vs 7 and 7 — Strong Hit with a match. +1 Momentum (capped at 10).*

The stones moved more easily than expected. The match: one of the blocking rocks was a standing stone — a marker pulled from the front of the tomb. It had runes Lewis couldn't read, and beside them a crude map of the tomb carved into the stone. A single passage. A chamber at the end. Something marked at its centre.

Lewis committed it to memory and stepped inside.

## The Tomb

**Face Danger — Shadow (1)**
*Action die: 6. Challenge dice: 8 and 3. Result: 7 vs 8 and 3 — Weak Hit. -1 Momentum (now 9).*

He moved slowly, one hand on the wall, waiting for his eyes to adjust. Nothing stirred. The passage was longer than the map suggested, or felt that way in the dark. He reached the chamber.

Small. Low-ceilinged. The air completely still.

In the centre, on a stone shelf: a sword. Older than anything Lewis had seen. Rusted nearly to nothing. Unmistakably a sword.

He stood in the entrance and looked at it.

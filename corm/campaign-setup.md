# Campaign Setup — Corm de Vela

# Inspirations & Themes — Corm de Vela Campaign

## Inspirations

*Strong:*
- El Cid (Cantar de Mio Cid) — exile structure, company building, pragmatic alliances, reputation as currency
- Between Two Fires (Thomas Drake) — moral compromise in a world rotting from the inside, the cost of surviving impossible circumstances
- Eisenhorn trilogy (Dan Abnett) — the inquisitor's dilemma, knowledge that looks like heresy, corruption as gradient

*Considered:*
- The Name of the Rose (Umberto Eco) — forbidden knowledge, institutional suppression, the horror of what the church refuses to acknowledge
- Parzival (Wolfram von Eschenbach) — a knight failing his vow through inaction, the wound that won't heal until the right question is asked
- Hellboy (Mignola) — a man who fights monsters and is accused of being one, folkloric horror grounded in exhausted professionalism
- The Witcher — the monster hunter outside both factions, trusted by neither, needed by both
- The Faithful and the Fallen (John Gwynne) — loyalty tested against institutional betrayal, genuine cosmic stakes
- Njáls saga — slow-burning feud logic, honour culture, one injustice generating the next
- Dune (Frank Herbert) — abilities that are illegible to institutions, knowledge that looks like witchcraft from outside
- The Road (Cormac McCarthy) — exhausted survival, moral erosion, the texture of a world that has lost most of itself
- Master and Margarity (Bulgakov) — the devil among us

## Historical Grounding

**El Cid (Rodrigo Díaz de Vivar, c.1043-1099)**
The structural spine. A loyal knight exiled on fabricated charges, building a company through pragmatic alliances across factional lines, eventually becoming powerful enough that the king who exiled him needs him more than he needs the king. Key moments that map onto the campaign:

- *First exile:* unjust, external, Corm still expects restoration
- *Company building:* men attach themselves to reputation; authority rests on winning and paying, not on title
- *Pragmatic alliances:* fighting for and against multiple factions, moral complexity accumulating
- *Second exile:* partly his own doing — acting without sanction because he had grown powerful enough not to need it; upstaging the king at the worst possible moment
- *Independent power:* becoming something that cannot be recalled, only reconciled with
- *Death and aftermath:* the thing he built does not outlast him

**The Knights Templar Dissolution (1307-1312)**
An institution destroyed through fabricated accusations of heresy and demonism by a king who owed them money. The accusations were almost certainly false. Nobody who mattered cared. Brother Vidal's report against Corm operates by the same logic — precise, moderate in tone, devastating in effect.

**Roger Bacon (c.1214-1292)**
A Franciscan friar imprisoned by his own order for knowledge that looked like heresy. Spent years writing encyclopedic works nobody was allowed to read. The Corm problem in historical form: a man whose abilities make him illegible and therefore threatening to normal power structures.

**Cicero's Exile (58 BC)**
A man whose greatest service to the state became the reason the state destroyed him. The gap between public rhetoric and private despair. The second exile as the more devastating because it contains complicity.

## Core Themes

**The accusation that fits**
Corm is accused of demonism. He is not a demonist. But the accusation fits the evidence — he knows things he shouldn't, he used methods that worked and looked wrong, he spoke a name that answered him. Brother Vidal's report was precise and opportunistic, not fabricated. The truth is more complicated than either innocence or guilt. Corm cannot fully refute the accusation because doing so would require explaining things that make him sound worse.

**Knowledge as the line**
Every protagonist in this campaign universe holds knowledge that institutions can't accommodate. For Corm this is explicit and personal — the knowledge came from his grandfather, from decades of private study, from a childhood encounter he has never fully explained even to himself. The Talisman asset is the most visible expression of it: he knows the devil's name because he has spent his life learning it, and that knowledge looks, from outside, like exactly what he is accused of.

**The vow against the self**
*I will not become what I am accused of being.* This is the background vow — Epic rank, never fulfilled, only kept. It is defensive rather than aspirational. Corm does not trust what he might become if he stopped holding the line. His emotional reserve is discipline. His precision is a form of vigilance. The question the campaign asks slowly and without announcing it: is the line he's holding real, or is he already further along the gradient than he knows?

**The exile as clarification**
The first exile strips away everything Corm built — position, household, the careful management of his secret. What remains is what he actually is. The campaign is the process of discovering what that is, which is not the same as what he thought it was.

**Brenna as the only complete witness**
Corm told Brenna about the childhood encounter. Nobody else knows. She holds the complete picture of him — not an idealised version, an accurate one, including the ways he is genuinely difficult. Her continued belief in him is not blind faith. It is an informed position, which makes it both more sustaining and more demanding. He has to be worth that position. The separation tests whether he is.

**The supernatural as personal**
Unlike Lewis's fifth season — a cosmic, communal crisis — Corm's supernatural threat is specific and personal. One devil, one man, a relationship that predates Corm's birth. The campaign's larger stakes (the coming force, the vanguard, the eventual army) are real, but they arrive late. For most of the campaign the horror is the thing that has been following him since childhood, operating in the margins, occasionally making itself felt. He is the only person equipped to fight it and the only person who knows it exists.

## Campaign Arc (El Cid Structure)

**Early arc** — first exile, company building. Lewis still expects restoration. The supernatural is quiet — present only as rumour, as the pattern Corm recognises and no one else does. Human threats: political, factional, the mechanics of surviving without a lord.

**Middle arc** — increasingly independent. Pragmatic alliances across lines that should not be crossed. The second exile as turning point — his own success the cause, the king's pride the mechanism. The supernatural begins to intrude: isolated encounters with the vanguard, things operating alone in the margins. Each one a data point in a pattern.

**Late arc** — independent power. Brenna joins him when it becomes clear restoration is not coming on his terms. The vanguard activity increasing, the pattern undeniable to anyone paying attention. The human political situation and the supernatural threat converging — the demonic has been steering the factional war.

**Endgame** — the armies clash. By this point the background vow of *I will not become what I am accused of being* has been tested to its limit. What does the line he has held actually mean, and has he held it?

## Tone Reference

**El Cid:** The arc is neither tragedy nor triumph but something more honest than either. A man who gets something like what he wanted at a cost he didn't anticipate, who builds something that doesn't outlast him, whose reputation exceeds his comfort with it. The tone is matter-of-fact about extraordinary things — the Vinland Sagas register, the extraordinary treated as logistics.

**Eisenhorn:** Accumulation rather than event. The fall in others' eyes tracks a man's necessary compromises. Each step looks reasonable from the previous step. The question of whether the line was crossed is genuinely ambiguous by the end, which is the point.

**Between Two Fires:** Survival in conditions that were not designed for survival. Moral compromise as the price of continuing. The world is rotting and the rot is not metaphorical. The protagonist is not heroic — they are persistent, which is harder and less glamorous.

## What Makes This Campaign Distinct from Lewis

Lewis operates externally — the institutions and systems around him are the problem, and his task is to work against them without losing his position. His Heart 3 means he leads through loyalty and presence. People follow him because he keeps his word.

Corm operates internally — the question is whether he becomes what he has been accused of being. His Wits 3 means he leads through precision and observation. People follow him because he wins and pays.

Lewis's horror is communal — the fifth season affects everyone. Corm's horror is personal — one creature, one vow, one man.

Lewis's world requires collaboration across enemy lines to survive. Corm's world requires him to survive long enough to understand what he is actually fighting — and whether fighting it will leave him recognisable.

## Setting

Loosely Iberian in flavour without being historically literal — the way *Between Two Fires* is French-feeling without being France. Geography, names, and texture are Iberian-adjacent; the world is invented freely within that register.

### The Spiritual Rot
The kingdom's religion holds that the king is the sacred bulwark against evil — the one figure whose authority and person stands between the mortal world and what presses against it from below. This role has not been practically tested in living memory. It is doctrine, theology, assumption.

It is about to be tested.

A supernatural force is gathering. Its vanguard has been operating in the margins for years — isolated incidents, things that don't quite fit, rumours that serious men dismiss. The full force is an endgame threat. For now it exists as absence and pressure: a devil working alone, a pattern that only one man alive has been trained to read.

### The Church
The institutional church acknowledges evil in the abstract but refuses to engage with it practically. Admitting that devils are real, that specific creatures can be named and warded against, would be at minimum a minor heresy — it implies knowledge the church doesn't sanction and methods it can't control. Official doctrine handles the supernatural through prayer, authority, and denial.

This makes practical knowledge of the occult dangerous to possess. It looks, from outside, exactly like what it fights.

### Character Notes

Corm is a precise, controlled man. His Wits are his primary weapon — he reads situations and people accurately, accumulates knowledge carefully, and acts on calculation rather than instinct. He is not warm. People follow him because he wins and pays, not because they love him.

His emotional reserve is not merely personality. It is discipline. He does not fully trust what he might become if he stopped holding the line against himself. The background vow is as much about containing himself as about resisting external corruption.

He is blunt to the point of rudeness without intending it. He does not perform loyalty or warmth. He keeps his word because his word is one of the few things he controls entirely.

## Vows

### Background Vow — *I will not become what I am accused of being* (Epic)
Corm was accused of demonism. He is not a demonist. But he carries knowledge that looks like communion from outside, and he knows — has always known since the childhood encounter — that the line between studying darkness and being consumed by it is real and requires constant tending.

This vow is defensive rather than aspirational. It is never fulfilled, only kept. Progress is marked by resistance: every time he holds the line, every time he has the means to cross it and doesn't.

The day it breaks, if it breaks, is the end of the story.

**Progress: 0/40**

### Inciting Vow — *I will restore my name and return for my family* (Formidable)
Corm has been exiled on a fabricated accusation of demonism. He left Brenna and their children at a monastery and rode away believing restoration is possible, perhaps inevitable. He does not yet know how long it will take or what it will cost.

**Progress: 0/10**

## Relationships

### Brenna de Vela (wife)
Knows Corm completely — including the childhood encounter with the devil, which he has never told anyone else. Her belief in him is not idealisation; it is honest knowledge of exactly who he is, flaws included, and a decision that he is worth it. She finds him genuinely difficult sometimes. She is his conscience precisely because she doesn't pretend otherwise.

Currently at a monastery with their children, waiting. She has her own threshold for how long she will wait before acting on it.

### Rodrigo de Vela (grandfather, deceased)
Minor church official — low enough never to attract scrutiny, connected enough to have access and knowledge. Raised Corm after his father's death. Was present at Corm's birth when the devil attacked; intervened somehow, and spent the rest of his life preparing Corm to survive.

Kept his occult knowledge secret because at the time even acknowledging that devils were real would have been considered heresy. Died of old age ten to fifteen years before the campaign begins. Did not tell Corm everything he knew — whether from caution or simply running out of time, Corm has never been able to decide.

The knowledge he passed on was thorough preparation for survival. It was not preparation for understanding.

### Brother Vidal (antagonist, ecclesiastical)
A church administrator with a legal mind and a political career. Was at the monastery during Corm's investigation on unrelated business, heard a recovered monk's account of the incident, and filed a precise, moderate, devastating report. He does not necessarily believe Corm is a demonist — he believes Corm is dangerous, unsanctioned, and that removing him weakens a rival.

### Ferran (antagonist, court)
Brother Vidal's brother. A minor court noble with ambition. Did not originate the accusation — saw his brother's report and recognised the weapon. Moved to place it in front of the king without Vidal fully understanding what he had set in motion. Vidal is now complicit in something he didn't quite choose.

### The Devil (supernatural antagonist)
A specific creature — a devil, vanguard of the supernatural force gathering at the edge of the story. Attacked Corm at birth and failed, for reasons neither Corm nor anyone living fully understands. Has been a presence at the edges of his life since. Is not random or opportunistic; it has a specific interest in Corm that predates his birth.

Corm has studied it for decades. He knows its name. He knows how to ward against it. He does not know why it came for him.

## The Accusations

**First exile** — fabricated. Corm investigated a supernatural incursion at a border monastery, dealt with it by methods that worked, and a recovering monk reported that he had *spoken to it* before destroying it — that he knew its name, that it answered him. Brother Vidal filed a careful report. Ferran weaponised it. The king exiled Corm on the evidence.

Corm did know the name. That knowledge came from decades of private scholarship, inherited from Rodrigo, built in secret. It looked exactly like what it wasn't.

**Second exile** — permanent, and partly his own doing. A supernatural incursion the king's forces were failing to contain. Corm acted, succeeded, and the story that spread was his story — because he was the one who stopped it. The king had to stand aside and watch. Kingship in this world is constitutive: the king's ability to protect his people is theological, not merely political. Corm didn't just upstage him. He unmade something essential about what the king is supposed to be.

The king exiled him again because acknowledging Corm would cost more than losing him. The second exile is permanent because Corm is now too dangerous to recall and too visible to ignore.

Corm understands this. He didn't intend it. He sees exactly what he did to the king by succeeding.

---

## The Opening

The campaign opens at the first exile — as the Cantar de Mio Cid opens, in medias res, with the protagonist already leaving.

Corm has said goodbye to Brenna and their children at a monastery. That farewell is offscreen — private, imperfect, probably not enough. He is riding away with a small retinue of men who believed him or had nowhere else to go. Warband strength: 2.

He believes restoration is possible. He has a vow and a direction. The supernatural is quiet for now — the devil does not announce itself in the opening sessions. The first threats are human: political, cold, a man rebuilding from nothing in a world that has decided he is dangerous.

## Open Questions
- Who are the named individuals in Corm's starting retinue?
- Which direction does he ride first, and toward what immediate objective?
- What does Corm's father's death mean to him — background fact or open wound?
- What did Rodrigo's intervention at Corm's birth actually look like?
- Why did the devil fail? Does anything survive that could answer this?
- What is Ferran's longer game — does he want Corm dead, or merely absent?
- How long will Brenna wait before she stops waiting?

# Session 3 Summary — Hammarby

## Where We Left Off

Lewis stands alone in a tomb in the forest northeast of Hammarby, looking at an ancient rusted sword on a stone shelf in the chamber at the end of a single passage. He does not know yet what it means or whether it is what Saoirse sent him to retrieve.

---

## What Happened This Session

The session opened at dawn with Edde and Rolf arguing over the prisoner — Edde wanting him hanged, Rolf holding the line until Lewis appeared and shut it down. Lewis kept Bram alive as a practical measure: he needed to know whether executing him would mean war, and whether a raid was coming. Both questions could only be answered by Conlaoch, who had conspicuously failed to mention the existence of a hostile faction when they spoke at the standing stone.

Lewis spent the morning in the village. He identified Oskar — a peasant with strips in the north field who lost his son in the Harrying — as the man who had been leaving food at the treeline for Conlaoch. Oskar didn't deny it. Edith arrived uninvited to state that Oskar had done nothing wrong, and handed Lewis a note: a formal invitation, written in the old tongue, to a meeting in a clearing northeast of the estate. Three days hence. The druid's handwriting, or someone close to her — the same hand as some of the old estate records.

Three days passed. Lewis attempted conversation with Bram each evening. Bram said nothing. On the third morning Lewis, Rolf, and Bally — with Bram slung over the horse's back — walked northeast into the forest. At the clearing, Saoirse was waiting with Conlaoch at her shoulder. Lewis sent Rolf back with the horse and the prisoner.

Saoirse explained the exchange: she would seal the north field if Lewis retrieved something from a tomb deeper in the forest — a place she could not enter herself. The dead there would not trouble a man who entered honestly. Between here and the tomb was a spring. The spring would ask something of him. She could not say what.

At the spring Lewis knelt and found himself thinking about the Harrying without meaning to — the violence he was complicit in, the things he did not stop, the weight he had been carrying under everything else. He looked at it directly for the first time. The spring asked nothing more than that. He stood up hollowed but cleaner.

The journey to the tomb was slow and disorienting. Lewis lost the path once in the mist and arrived rattled, spirit worn. At the tomb entrance he found the passage recently sealed — stones packed in, a warning charm across the lintel. Conlaoch's work, or done at his direction. Someone who knew the tradition would have turned back. Lewis moved the stones.

Inside, the tomb was small and still. A single passage, a chamber at the end. On a stone shelf in the centre: a sword. Ancient, rusted nearly to nothing. Unmistakably a sword.

He is standing in front of it now.

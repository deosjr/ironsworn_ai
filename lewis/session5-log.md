# Session 5 Log — Hammarby
*An Ironsworn solo campaign. Lewis of Mervik, lord of Hammarby.*

---

## The Field

The first morning, Oskar led a handful of peasants onto the north field with a borrowed plough and two of the village's oxen. Lewis watched from the hall's doorway, then went inside. He didn't go back to watch. Either it worked or it didn't.

It worked. The tilling took three days. Nobody made anything of it. They just worked the way they'd been unable to work for two seasons, quietly, like men who had decided not to think too hard about what had changed.

On the second day Oskar turned up something in the furrow — Lewis didn't see it happen, but he saw Oskar crouch and pocket something in one motion without breaking stride. He did not mention it to Lewis that evening. Lewis didn't ask.

---

## The Boundary Dispute

It came from the west end of the village. Two families — the Knutssons and old Bernt — arguing over three feet of ground that had been disputed before Lewis arrived, probably before the Harrying, possibly before anyone living could say when. The Knutssons' elder son had been driving stakes at night. Bernt's wife had pulled them up. Someone's hand had been cut, or said to be cut, though the wound was not in evidence when Lewis looked.

He heard both sides on the same afternoon so neither had time to prepare. He gave the three feet to Bernt, for the only reason he had: Bernt's family had been on that side of it longer, the records Theodor had found — old, partial, in the native tongue — suggested as much, and the Knutssons' son had driven the stakes in the dark. He said so plainly. He did not mention the son's name.

The Knutssons accepted it the way people accept rulings they intend to revisit. The son looked at Lewis the whole time and said nothing.

## Maret

Lewis noticed it on the fourth morning — a small bunch of meadow grass tied with a strip of linen, left at the corner post of the north field's fence line. He noticed it again the fifth morning, and the sixth. He never caught Maret placing it. He stopped checking what it was.

---

## The Sword

He did it at the end of the week. The blade had been wiped down after the tomb but not properly attended to. Lewis sat by the fire with whetstone, oil, and cloth, and worked through it the way you do a thing that doesn't need to be talked about. An hour, maybe more. The steel came back to itself.

The sword had been in a lake. It had been in a tomb. Whatever walked out of that tomb had put a hand on Lewis and the sword had put it down. That seemed like enough of a relationship to tend carefully.

---

## The Charm

The woven charm went up on the wall by the door to Lewis's room — the one piece of the hall that was fully enclosed now. A nail already there, left by whoever had lived there before. Lewis put the charm on it without thinking about it too hard. It was placed as a warning; it had warned too late; he read it as Aldric's name now and let it be that.

---

## Theodor

They talked three nights after the boundary dispute, when Lewis had stopped turning the Knutsson ruling over and asking himself whether he'd gotten it right.

Lewis told him about the spring. Not everything — not the exact shape of it — but enough. That the forest had a way of making you think about things you had stopped thinking about. That he had thought about the Harrying, standing by that water. What he had done and what he had let happen and how long he had been filing it somewhere he didn't have to look.

Theodor listened. He did not offer anything. When Lewis stopped talking the fire went on a while.

Then Theodor said: *"You are still here. That is something. Most men who did what you did are still doing it."*

Lewis had no answer to that. It wasn't quite absolution and it wasn't quite accusation. It sat in the room and neither of them touched it.

---

## The Survey

A rider came through from the south on market day — not stopping, just passing — and Theodor caught a word about the queen's census agents being active in the region two baronies over. Closer than the last report. He told Lewis that evening with the same tone he used for everything, which meant Lewis listened carefully.

Theodor had the soil drainage report ready. Lewis asked if it would hold. Theodor said it would hold anyone who didn't already know to look for something. He left the question of what happened when someone who *did* know arrived unspoken between them.

---

## Saoirse

The morning after the survey news, Edith appeared at Lewis's door at first light with a dead crow. Not freshly dead — preserved somehow, dry and neat, with a strip of bark tied to one leg with markings cut into it.

Lewis brought it to Edith, who read the marks without touching the bark and told him: a place, and a number of days, and a direction. Northeast. Past the spring. Seven days' time.

Lewis stood in her doorway and looked at the crow in his hand and thought about a queen's surveyor who might arrive any day, and a place northeast of the estate he was expected at in seven days, and said nothing for long enough that Edith told him to close the door, the cold was coming in.

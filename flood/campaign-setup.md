## The World

### The Three Periods

**Antediluvian**
Almost entirely unknown. Fragments only — a carved stone recovered from the deep, a technique nobody alive invented, a word in a dead language that appears in three separate island cultures that have had no contact. The picture assembles slowly and is never complete. What it suggests is more unsettling than what it confirms.

The myths of every surviving culture converge on the same shape: hubris, digging too deep, unlocking powers that could not be contained, Faustian bargains. The specifics differ. The shape is always the same. Multiple cultures with no post-flood contact independently preserving the same warnings suggests the warnings are true.

The antediluvians went too far in some direction — depth, knowledge, power, bargaining — and something responded. The flood was a consequence, possibly a containment measure, possibly a punishment, possibly all three simultaneously. The ruins are not just archaeological. They are evidence of what happens at the end of that road.

Critically: the antediluvians went *down* as well as across. Ships have been found built for depth rather than surface — reinforced against pressure, hatches designed to hold against force from outside, windows too thick for weather. They were descending into deep water deliberately and repeatedly. Whatever they found there was worth returning to, or required regular attention. The Faustian bargain element of the flood myth has a location.

**The Flood and Immediate Aftermath**
The water is mostly what exists. Islands of survivors, each community shaped by what they managed to carry or remember or accidentally preserve. Travel between islands is the most important thing in the world — connection is the only thing preventing complete fragmentation. This period is living memory for the oldest cultures, myth for most.

Technology largely lost. Shipbuilding reduced to fishing boats. The knowledge of deep-water vessels exists only in fragments: a joint technique in a waterlogged wreck, a rigging arrangement carved into a temple wall, a word in the old language that translates roughly as *the kind of ship that goes far.*

**The Postdiluvian Present**
The water is receding. Fast — not geological slowness but visibly, within a human lifetime, within the campaign itself. New land surfaces. Drowned cities become accessible. The world is actively changing during the campaign.

The postdiluvian powers did not plan for this. They built their institutions on the assumption that the ocean floor stays where it is. The recession catches everyone off guard. The explorer's charter exists because someone was quick enough to see the opportunity — but the framework is improvised, the maps are wrong, and nobody knows what's coming up next.

Something that's inaccessible at campaign start is above water by the end. Something that surfaces in act two should have stayed submerged.

### Water and Culture

The relationship with water defines almost all cultures still in existence. The flood is not just historical — it is the central fact of every mythology, every taboo, every social structure. How a culture relates to the water tells you everything about who they are.

Open water crossing is taboo in most island cultures — not merely dangerous but forbidden. The taboo emerged because early attempts went badly. People came back changed, or came back with things that shouldn't have come back, or didn't come back but their boats did. The monster story simplified this into something actionable. The actual version is harder to communicate and harder to obey.

---

### The Federation

A sea-going federation has already formed in the wider world — your protagonist discovers this at the end of the early arc, when they believed themselves to be pioneers. The federation has routes, rules, territories, relationships with certain islands and not others.

The federation is aware of what is surfacing from the antediluvian and is exploiting it. They have a framework for what the material is and what it's for. That framework is wrong in ways they don't know yet, and they are building on it aggressively. The danger is not malice — it is confident incorrectness at scale. They have already had casualties they have explained away. Finds that didn't behave as expected. People who came back changed. The official story smooths these over.

The protagonist's home island is outside the federation's structure — a disadvantage and a freedom simultaneously. They can go where the federation won't, deal with who the federation doesn't, find what the federation isn't looking for.

## The Protagonist's Origin

### The Island Cluster
A group of islands close enough together that short crossings between them gave the inhabitants practical seamanship that single-island cultures never developed. Not deep water voyaging — but enough to build on.

More importantly: a strong oral tradition that remembers more than it should. Not through preserved texts or artifacts but through talking — the tradition maintained a picture of a larger world, other peoples, other ways of doing things. Everyone else on the cluster treats this as myth. The protagonist treats it as a destination.

The tradition has gaps. Distortions. Things that got simplified over generations into something manageable. It preserved the shape of what came before but lost the content. The protagonist has been pattern-matching against it their whole life — and the pattern keeps almost fitting things they find, in ways that accumulate.

### The Big Ship
The community invests collectively in building a vessel capable of longer crossings — everyone chips in, everyone is invested in what it finds. The protagonist must establish proof of concept first with a modest vessel, then bring back something valuable enough to justify the larger commitment.

The ship the protagonist eventually builds is more capable than it should be by island standards. Some of what they incorporated from wreck fragments — joints, sealing techniques, pressure fittings — came from antediluvian deep-water vessels without the protagonist fully understanding this. The ship behaves differently in deep water than it should. This has not yet become apparent.

## The Last Keeper

Found at the end of the early arc — the first significant find, and the thing that justifies the community's investment in the big ship.

Not a castaway. A person who was running. Their island is gone — no longer above water, or emptied over generations, or both. They stayed as long as staying was possible. They are the last living container of what their island knew, aware of that fact, carrying it alone.

They were running from the federation, who wanted what they carry. Their arrival at the protagonist's home island was not entirely accidental — their own tradition mentioned the island cluster in terms they didn't fully understand until they arrived and heard the oral tradition echo back at them.

They do not mention the federation to the community. Keeping the community in the dark suits them — ignorance is a form of protection, and a community that doesn't know about the federation can't make decisions that attract the federation's attention. This is a unilateral decision made on behalf of people who didn't consent to it. They tell the protagonist privately, eventually. The protagonist then faces the same choice.

Their presence makes the protagonist's home island geopolitically significant whether anyone there knows it or not.

## Islands

### Abandoned Islands

**The Counting Island**
Every flat surface covered in marks. Tallies at first glance — but the groupings are wrong for counting. Too large, too irregular, following patterns that suggest something other than inventory. The island was inhabited long enough to cover every rock face, every wall, every floor. Nobody here now. Whatever they were counting, they never stopped.

*The adventure:* figuring out what was being counted, and whether the count was finished.
*The dark edge:* the marks are still being added to. Faintly. Recently.

---

**The Prepared Island**
Someone left this place in order. Stores carefully sealed, tools hung up, fires banked. Not abandoned in crisis — vacated deliberately, with the intention of returning. The intention was generations ago. Everything is still waiting.

*The adventure:* where did they go, and why haven't they come back.
*The dark edge:* the stores are still fresh. The fires are still warm.

---

**The Wrong Geometry Island**
Structures that predate the flood, partially exposed by the recession. The architecture is antediluvian — built to proportions that don't quite match human occupation. Not dramatically wrong. Just slightly off in ways that accumulate. Doorframes a little too wide, stairs with a riser height that doesn't match any comfortable stride, windows positioned to look at nothing in particular from inside but clearly oriented toward something specific that is now underwater.

*The adventure:* what were these built for, and by whom.
*The dark edge:* the orientation of the windows. What they're pointing at is now surfacing.

---

**The Vault**
A structure that emerged from the recession almost completely intact — sealed, engineered, built to last. The antediluvians made it to survive exactly what happened. Which means they knew what was coming.

The outside is featureless except for a single mechanism — a calendar, essentially, counting down since before the flood. It has recently — weeks, perhaps days — reached zero and opened itself.

*The adventure:* what was preserved inside, and for whom. The vault wasn't built as a tomb or a cache. It was built as a message. The intended recipient is unclear.
*The dark edge:* something got in before you. Or something got out.

---

**The Abandoned Arsenal**
An island that is almost entirely storage — racks, sealed containers, infrastructure for maintaining and distributing things. Military in organisation. The arsenal wasn't abandoned in crisis. It was methodically emptied — whatever was stored here was taken somewhere before the flood. The logistics of the operation are still readable: manifest systems, routing marks, a chain of distribution that points toward a destination.

*The adventure:* following the distribution chain to understand what was moved and where it went.
*The dark edge:* one section was sealed differently — sealed to keep something in. It is still sealed. The manifest lists its contents in a script the protagonist can almost read. The word appearing most frequently translates to something between *last resort* and *do not.*

---

### Inhabited Islands

**The Silence Island**
A thriving community that communicates entirely without spoken language — an elaborate system of gesture, expression, and written marks. They are not mute by incapacity. Speech is taboo. Has been for as long as anyone can remember, for reasons the oldest members communicate with great difficulty and evident discomfort.

*The adventure:* trade is possible but hard. Trust is possible but slow. Eventually someone communicates why speaking is forbidden — or tries to, and can't find the signs for it.
*The dark edge:* the taboo exists because something on this island responds to human voices. It has been quiet for a long time. The crew will struggle to stay silent.

---

**The Debtor Island**
Prosperous, organised, apparently thriving. Every family keeps meticulous records of what they owe — not to each other, not to any institution. To something referred to only as *the prior claim.* Regular offerings made to the water at specific tidal intervals. Nobody will explain what the prior claim is, only that it predates the island, predates the flood, and has never been late in collecting.

*The adventure:* trade negotiations that keep bumping against obligations the islanders won't explain. Something is owed on a schedule and the timing of the visit is not coincidental.
*The dark edge:* the prior claim notices the ship. It is not in the ledger. That is a problem.

---

**The Remembering Island**
An oral tradition so detailed and so well-maintained that they can tell you the name of every person who lived on their island for thirty generations back. They remember everything. They forget nothing. It is considered the highest virtue and the heaviest burden.

When the protagonist's oral tradition comes up in conversation, the eldest rememberer goes very still. Then asks a question containing a word from the protagonist's tradition that nobody outside their home island should know.

*The adventure:* what is the connection, and how deep does it go.
*The dark edge:* they remember things that haven't happened yet. They've been expecting the protagonist. They don't seem pleased about it.

---

**The Volcano Island**
An island that should be uninhabitable — active, venting, bad air in certain winds. Inhabited by a community that has lived on its slopes for as long as their tradition records. They consider the volcano a relationship, not a hazard. They have maintained it, tended it, given it things at regular intervals. In exchange it has not killed them.

The recession has exposed the volcano's lower slopes for the first time. What's down there — preserved in volcanic rock, sealed in lava flows older than the flood — is intact in a way nothing else found has been.

*The adventure:* negotiating access to what's exposed against a community that has a prior claim and a functional relationship with something that can kill everyone on the island.
*The dark edge:* what the community has been giving the volcano. The ledger of that relationship goes back further than their oral tradition. Something was already here when their ancestors arrived.

---

**The Dragon's Lair**
No dragon. But something lives here — large, old, territorial — and the island has been shaped around its presence long enough that the relationship is architectural. Paths avoid certain areas. Structures built to certain heights but no higher. Vegetation wrong in a rough circle at the island's center.

The island is uninhabited by people. Very clearly inhabited.

*The adventure:* what is it, and is trade possible. The recession has exposed new territory the creature has not yet established a relationship with. That window may be brief.
*The dark edge:* the creature is antediluvian. Not a monster — a remnant. Something made for a purpose, fulfilling that purpose alone since the flood, with nobody left to report to. It recognises the ship. Specifically. The ship design, assembled from wreck fragments, matches something in its memory. It has been waiting for that ship to come back.

## Open Water

### Sea Creatures

Most things encountered at sea are large, strange, and indifferent. A shape beneath the hull that doesn't behave like a whale. A disturbance that follows the ship for a day and then doesn't. Something that surfaces once, registers the ship, and descends again. These are assessments, not attacks.

The genuinely dangerous encounters are rare and specific — not random predation but something like a response. The water isn't full of monsters waiting to eat ships. The ones that care, care for reasons.

**Territorial creatures** cluster around specific coordinates corresponding to antediluvian structures on the ocean floor. Not guarding exactly — maintaining a relationship with a location. The ship passing through is an interruption.

**Recognising creatures** respond to the ship specifically — the design, the materials, the antediluvian fragments incorporated without full understanding. Recognition rather than aggression, which is somehow worse. Being recognised by something that old implies a history you didn't know you had.

**Degraded creatures** are wrong in a way that suggests they weren't always what they are now. Proportions off. Behaviour fragmented, repetitive — something following instructions it no longer fully understands. Not dangerous so much as implicating. Something made them, or something happened to them, and the result is still here long after the cause is gone.

---

### Things That Look Like Ships

**Wrecks and debris** that from a distance have the profile of something intact. Navigational hazards and occasional treasure.

**The unmanned ships** — something that moves with purpose, holds a heading, responds to wind correctly. From a distance indistinguishable from a vessel under sail. Up close: wrong. Antediluvian construction, intact in a way nothing that old should be. No crew visible. Movement that doesn't quite match the wind. Going somewhere specific, has been going there for a very long time. Following a route that predates the flood, calling at ports now underwater, completing a journey that has no destination anymore.

Sighting it once is a mystery. Sighting it twice means it's on a route. Sighting it a third time means your routes and its routes intersect for a reason. Boarding it is a session in itself.

**The convergence** — two unmanned ships, same profile, moving toward each other from opposite horizons. Meeting at a specific point. Whatever happens at the meeting point happens below the surface — the ships descend together, smoothly, as if the water is the destination rather than the obstacle. The protagonist watches from a distance and must decide whether to follow the coordinates or pretend they didn't see it.

---

### The Dead Zone

An area of water the protagonist's oral tradition mentions obliquely — a term translating roughly as *where the water remembers.* No specific warning. Just a name.

Sailing into it: nothing attacks. Nothing surfaces. The water glassy regardless of wind. Sound behaves differently — voices carry too far, then not far enough. The crew gets quiet without deciding to. The ship's antediluvian components respond to something — a vibration in the hull that shouldn't be there, tension in lines not under load.

You pass through and nothing happens. The crew is quieter on the other side and nobody wants to say why.

On the return journey something is different. The water remembers the ship now. It remembers you were here. Whether that matters depends on what's listening.

## Structural Notes

### Campaign Arc

**Early arc** — proof of concept crossings in a modest vessel. First island contacts. The last keeper found and brought home. The community's investment in the big ship justified. Ends with the discovery that the federation exists and has been building infrastructure while the protagonist was making first crossings. The protagonist learns this privately from the keeper. The community does not.

**Mid campaign** — the Firefly structure operational. Crew assembled from different island cultures, each carrying a different fragment of the flood mythology. Jobs from people not entirely trusted, in places that don't entirely want them. The antediluvian pattern assembling itself around the protagonist whether they're looking for it or not. The federation's interests beginning to conflict with the protagonist's findings.

**Late campaign** — the pattern undeniable. What the antediluvians were doing, what the flood actually was, what's still down there. The federation's confident incorrectness at scale becoming a crisis. The KOTAR-style revelation that recontextualises everything before it.

**Endgame** — something surfaces that was supposed to stay submerged. The protagonist decides what to do with what they know.

### The Oral Tradition as Degraded Memory
The protagonist's cultural memory is the KOTOR amnesia reframed collectively. They have names for things before they find them — not accurate names, but close enough to recognise. The tradition preserved the shape but lost the content. Each significant find is a moment of recognition that reveals the tradition was always approximation. This happens once early and feels like discovery. By the third or fourth time it feels like something else.

### The Ship
The ship is the companion asset — its condition matters, its capabilities develop as the protagonist learns and rebuilds, losing it is a campaign-level crisis. Every improvement comes from something recovered, which means every improvement is also a data point in the antediluvian pattern. The ship is a museum of lost knowledge that is slowly becoming something else.

### The Crew
Each crew member comes from a different island culture. Each carries a different fragment of the flood mythology. The ship is a floating collection of contradictory oral traditions that slowly start rhyming. Nobody notices at first. The crew represents different relationships to the federation — deserter, true believer gone skeptical, someone who never encountered it, someone who owes it a debt.

- What is the federation called, and who leads it?
- What is the KOTOR-style revelation — what did the antediluvians actually do?
- What surfaces in the endgame that should have stayed submerged?
- What is in the sealed section of the abandoned arsenal?
- What is the prior claim on the debtor island?

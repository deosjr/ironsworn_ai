# WORLD DOCUMENT — Ironsworn Campaign

## Concept
A dark historical fantasy blending Roman Britain (~100AD) with post-Conquest England (~1070AD). Two cultural strata exist:
- **The Conquerors** (Roman/Norman blend): civic polytheism, state priests, pragmatic and militaristic, recently victorious
- **The Natives** (Celtic/Anglo-Saxon blend): old gods, druids, scattered and resentful, holders of deep mythic knowledge

The world is based loosely on the historical Harrying of the North, reframed as a fantasy setting. Geography resembles central Sweden (Uppsala region) rather than northern England — low rolling landscape, mixed forest, lakes, open fields.

---

## The Mythic Crisis: The Fifth Season

The native culture practiced **sacred kingship**: each year the king ritually wed a chosen maiden. The maiden was sacrificed in autumn to ensure the seasonal cycle continued. The king himself was sacrificed only when disaster struck (crop failure etc). This ritual kept the boundary between the living world and the underworld sealed.

The new Conqueror king refused to participate — the marriage, the sacrifice, and especially his own potential death are all unacceptable to him. He has declared total victory after the Harrying and considers the matter closed.

As a result, the seasonal cycle is breaking down. A **Fifth Season** — of endless mist — is growing longer each year. In myth this has happened before, but no living person has seen it or knows how it ended. The fifth season causes the boundary between the living world and the underworld to dissolve. Strange, unknowable things cross over — more Lovecraftian than corporeal. Ghosts, presences, things that should not be here. Not zombies or tangible undead, at least not yet.

The druids still perform the maiden ritual, knowing it is insufficient without the full sacred kingship cycle. They are messengers and interpreters of the gods, not power brokers — they have no hidden agenda beyond fulfilling their religious duty.

## Factions

### The Conqueror King
Declared victory. Willfully blind to the mythic crisis. Considers the old religion barbaric. Will resist any information contradicting his narrative of total control.

### The Queen
The most politically dangerous figure. Does not share her husband's complacency. Has caught wind of occult whispers and believes the druids represent a real threat — framing it as political rather than cosmic. Under cover of a national survey (equivalent of the Domesday Book), she is sending agents across the land to gather intelligence and wage a shadow war against the druids. Her inquisition is real and growing. She is a believer, but probably does not understand the true nature of what she is fighting.

### The Druids
Keepers of the old ritual. They choose the maiden each year, perform the ceremonies, and watch the fifth season worsen. They are not villains — they are functionaries of something larger than themselves, increasingly desperate. They are deeply resentful of the conquest and the Harrying. They would collaborate with the Conquerors to avert the fifth season if trust were possible, but it is not — yet.

### The Displaced Native Army
North of the border. Resentful, eager to reconquer their homeland, but currently powerless to act. A pressure that could become kinetic.

### The Rejected Maiden
Each year the druids still elect a maiden and perform what rituals they can, knowing it is insufficient without the king's participation. The maiden is still sacrificed in autumn. However, without the full sacred kingship cycle being observed, the local population increasingly questions the purpose — rumours circulate that the druids are simply abducting young women. At any time there is at most one living maiden, in the druids' keeping until autumn.

## Religion

### Conqueror Religion
Civic polytheism modeled on Roman practice. Priests are ritual administrators rather than spiritual guides — managing sacrifice, augury, temple ceremonies. The state cult practices *interpretatio* — absorbing native gods by renaming them as equivalents of their own pantheon. Tolerates old gods as long as new names are used and official priests are acknowledged. This makes druidic resistance simultaneously religious and political.

### Native Religion
Old gods, seasonal cycles, sacred kingship. The druids are interpreters and ritual specialists, not rulers. The religion is deeply tied to the land and its cycles.

## The Estate

### Situation
A ruined estate on the northern border, recently granted to the player character. Once prosperous, now largely destroyed by the Harrying. The new lord is expected to rebuild it from nothing.

The fifth season is already affecting it: crops failing in certain fields, peasants refusing to till specific areas due to ghostly presence or supernatural unease. Nothing the lord believes in yet.

### Geography
Modeled on the landscape around Linnaeus' Hammarby, southeast of Uppsala: low rolling terrain, mixed forest, open fields between tree lines, lakes. Quiet and melancholy.

**Langsjön** — a long narrow lake at one edge of the estate. The estate sits at one end; the far end disappears into forest and mist. Water is liminal in native myth; the druids likely have a relationship with this lake. Things could approach from the far end unseen.

Forest presses in from the north and west, thickening toward the border. The druids operate within it.

### Estate Inventory (at start of play)
- Burned hall: stone foundation intact, timber structure destroyed
- Small village of native peasants (~30-40), enough to work the land in theory
- Retinue of 4-6 soldiers, loyal to the lord personally
- Two clercs: one older administrator, one younger scribe (represent official religion and bureaucracy)
- Supplies for approximately one season
- One horse (lord cannot ride well — was a foot soldier, knighthood is a new and uncomfortable status)

### Feudal Obligations
The lord owes feudal levy to a nearby Norman baron, who is himself managing ruined estates and not yet collecting taxes. The levy for an estate this size is effectively just the lord himself — expected to arrive with horse and armour. He has the horse. The armour is a future problem.

---

## Key Relationships

### The Saved General
The lord distinguished himself saving this general in combat, which earned him the estate. The general is now managing multiple estates in the south and has largely forgotten about him. A connection that exists but is effectively dormant.

### The Local Baron
Immediate feudal superior. Dealing with his own reconstruction problems. Not yet demanding taxes or levy. Relationship not yet developed.

---

## Tragic Structure

The core tragedy: averting the fifth season requires collaboration between Conquerors and natives. The druids have the knowledge; the king has the power to restore the ritual. But the conquest and Harrying have made trust nearly impossible. The lord, positioned between both worlds as a lowborn soldier elevated into Norman nobility, may be the only person capable of eventually brokering this — if he survives long enough to understand what is actually needed.
